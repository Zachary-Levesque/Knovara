# Problem

## Engineers Spend Too Much Time Becoming Productive

When engineers join a new company, team, or project, they often spend weeks or months becoming productive.

The challenge is not simply learning new technologies or technical concepts.

The challenge is understanding the organization.

New engineers must figure out:

* What the company does
* How their team contributes
* What projects are important
* Which technologies matter
* What skills they need
* Which documentation is relevant
* Where information is stored
* Who owns specific systems
* Who to ask for help
* Why previous decisions were made

The information already exists, but it is scattered across:

* GitHub repositories
* Documentation
* Jira tickets
* Slack conversations
* Internal wikis
* Design documents
* Meetings
* Tribal knowledge held by experienced employees

As a result, engineers spend significant time searching for information, asking repetitive questions, and trying to understand how everything fits together.

## Consequences

### For New Engineers

* Slow ramp-up
* Information overload
* Uncertainty about priorities
* Delayed contributions
* Reduced confidence

### For Senior Engineers

* Frequent interruptions
* Repeated onboarding explanations
* Knowledge silos
* Reduced productivity

### For Organizations

* Longer onboarding periods
* Slower time-to-productivity
* Increased training costs
* Lost organizational knowledge

## Core Insight

The problem is not a lack of information.

The problem is that organizational knowledge is fragmented and difficult to navigate.

Engineers do not need more information.

They need guidance.

They need to know:

* What matters
* Why it matters
* Who can help
* What to learn next

## Opportunity

Create a system that transforms fragmented organizational knowledge into personalized technical mentorship.

Instead of forcing engineers to search for answers, guide them through the knowledge, people, projects, and decisions that are most relevant to their role.

The goal is simple:

> Make engineers become engineers faster.
